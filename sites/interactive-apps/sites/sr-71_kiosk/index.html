Cockpits

Seats:

  • The SR-71 features two cockpits with one seat each
  • The front cockpit was used for the pilot and the rear cockpit was used for the navigator
  • On training models, the rear cockpit was raised. This would be where the instructor would be located

Life Support:

  • Specialized protective, pressurized suits, similar to suits worn by astronauts, due to high altitudes
  • Onboard oxygen supply to keep the suit pressurized during the descent in cases of an emergency ejection
  • Food and drink provided during long reconaissance flights and was guided into an opening in the helmet

Sensors:

  • Optical/Infrared Imagery
  • Side-Looking Airborne Radar (SLAR)
  • Electronic Intelligence Gathering Systems (ELINT)

Airframe

Material:

  • Use of titanium was limited to components exposed to the highest temperatures, due to cost
  • Titanium was used for 85% of the structure. The rest was made using polymer composite materials
  • Major sections of the skin of the inboard wings were corrugated, not smooth. This allowed them to expand horizontally and vertically at high temperatures, increasing longitudinal strength

Shape:

  • The SR-71 was used for long, stealthy, reconaissance missions and needed to be able to fly undetected
  • The SR-71 features flattened, tapering sides that reflect most energy away from a radar beam's place of origin
  • Special radar-absorbing materials were incorporated into sawtooth-shaped sections of the aircraft's skin

Engines

Specs:

  • The SR-71 was powered by two Pratt & Whitney J58 axial-flow turbojet engines
  • The Blackbird could reach speeds over Mach 3 and the fastest reported speed was 2,193 mph
  • Maximum flight speed was limited by the temperature of the air entering the engine compressor, which was not certified for temperatures above 800°F

Air Inlets:

  • The air inlets allowed the SR-71 to cruise at over Mach 3.2, with the air slowing down to subsonic speed as it entered the engine
  • Mach 3.2 was the design point for the aircraft, its most efficient speed, however, in practice it was sometimes more efficient at even higher speeds

Landing:

Parachute:

  • The SR-71 landed at over 170 knots (200mph)
  • In order for the aircraft to land at high speeds, a drag parachute deployed from a hatch on top of the plane
  • The chute also acted to reduce stress on the tires

Tires:

  • The SR-71's tires were manufactured by B.F. Goodrich, contained aluminum, and were filled with nitrogen
  • They cost $2300 and would generally require replacing within 20 missions
